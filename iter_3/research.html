Critical review of iter_3/ as a quant research workflow

iter_3 is a compact autonomous macro-ETF strategy research harness. It is strongest as a controlled sandbox for fast, auditable, rule-based ETF rotation experiments. It is not yet a robust institutional quant research platform: its validation protocol is thin, its current results are heavily exposed to multiple-testing and recent-regime overfit, transaction costs are absent, and several documents/tests still encode older OOS-selection language that conflicts with the intended validation-CAGR-without-ruin objective.

Bottom line: Suitable for early-stage quant ideation and reproducible toy-to-small research loops, especially for long-only liquid ETF momentum. Not suitable, without material upgrades, for deciding live capital allocation, estimating durable alpha, comparing complex hypotheses, or running high-confidence macro/text research. Treat the current best strategy as a candidate anomaly to stress-test, not as a discovered production edge.

What this system actually is

The repository defines a single-file strategy research loop around a fixed backtest/evaluation engine:

  • prepare.py is the fixed engine: data loading, Yahoo price fetching/caching, monthly backtest, split evaluation, DBMF benchmark comparison, CAGR calculation, gross-exposure validation, and ruin detection.
  • train.py is the editable strategy file: it declares the ETF universe, signal parameters, weight selector, rationale metadata, and ledger appending.
  • ideas/schema.py enforces a human-auditable strategy rationale and the unified tab-separated experiment ledger schema.
  • datasets/ registers public data sources and contains partial ingestion/feature helpers for prices, FOMC text, Beige Book text, SEC EDGAR metadata, and FRED URL construction.
  • tests/ checks core invariants: no prior-day lookahead in monthly signal calls, DBMF matched-window handling, OOS split reporting, rationale validation, and text-feature lagging.
  • analysis.ipynb and analysis.executed.ipynb summarize the ledger, select the highest non-ruined validation-CAGR row, and generate progress.png.

Evaluation protocol and an important objective mismatch

The intended research charter in program.md is now maximize validation CAGR without ruin; locked OOS is a final diagnostic/generalization test, and DBMF excess is diagnostic only. train.py implements its own ledger status accordingly: keep requires non-ruin, positive validation CAGR, and validation CAGR above BEST_VALIDATION_CAGR.

However, there is a notable inconsistency:

  • README.md still says the objective is to rank by locked out-of-sample CAGR and keep only if an OOS gate passes.
  • prepare.py::passes_oos_gate still prioritizes oos_cagr > previous_best_oos_cagr, positive DBMF excess, positive validation, and an OOS/train degradation rule.
  • tests/test_oos.py explicitly names test_passes_oos_gate_prioritizes_oos_cagr, preserving the older objective.
  • train.py prints oos_passed from that legacy gate, while its ledger status uses the newer validation-CAGR rule.

This does not invalidate the current ledger status because append_current_result computes keep/discard independently from validation CAGR. But it is dangerous for autonomous agents and future users: a tool or notebook that trusts oos_passed or README wording could accidentally select on the locked OOS set, leaking the test set into research decisions.

What seems to have driven the winning result

  • Fast relative momentum dominated. 21-day lookbacks outperformed most 42/63/126-day variants on validation in this ledger.
  • Sector/thematic granularity mattered. The expanded universe includes semiconductors, technology, biotech, sector ETFs, commodities, gold, bonds, credit, factors, and cash. The top holdings by monthly selection frequency for the current strategy were SMH, XLE, SOXX, XBI, GLD, XLU, DBC, XLF, TLT, IWM, XLY, and XLK.
  • Top-two diversification helped slightly on validation. Top-one 21-day sector/style momentum had 32.017% validation CAGR; equal-weight top-two had 33.049%; score-weighted top-two reached 33.355%.
  • Several “risk control” variants reduced validation CAGR. SPY guard, trend filters, volatility target, and volatility-adjusted scoring mostly failed to beat the best validation row, though they may still be valuable under a drawdown-aware objective.
  • The high OOS result is probably regime-sensitive. 2025-2026 OOS performance is exceptional and likely reflects strong recent leadership/chasing behavior in specific ETF sleeves, not proof of a stable structural edge.

Major weaknesses and research risks

1. Multiple-testing and agentic overfit risk are high

The ledger has 41 formal trials, and the strategy class is a family of ETF-universe/lookback/top-N/filter/sizing variants. The selected validation CAGR is the maximum of many related trials on one two-year validation period. A high validation CAGR after iterative search is therefore biased upward. There is no deflated Sharpe, White’s Reality Check, combinatorial purged cross-validation, nested walk-forward, parameter-neighborhood stability test, or holdout embargo beyond the single locked OOS window.

2. The validation/OOS split is short and regime-specific

Train is 2020-2022, validation is 2023-2024, and locked OOS is 2025-present. That is a very small number of independent market regimes, especially for monthly ETF rotation. The OOS window is roughly 17 months in the observed run and can be dominated by a few leadership episodes. This is not enough to infer durable alpha.

3. Costs, taxes, and execution are omitted

The strategy has high monthly turnover by a simple weight-change proxy. The backtest assumes adjusted-close returns and immediate frictionless monthly rebalances. It does not model bid/ask spread, close execution, slippage on sector/thematic ETFs, taxable distributions, short-term capital gains, or account constraints. A real quant research platform needs a cost model and sensitivity table before interpreting CAGR.

4. Risk is under-specified

Ruin only means equity at/below 25% or invalid values. The final strategy’s observed max drawdown is about -33%, which is not “ruin” but is material for a retail account. The objective intentionally ignores Sharpe, drawdown, Calmar, CVaR, and turnover during selection. That is consistent with the program, but it means the harness is optimizing a narrow and potentially fragile property.

5. Data engineering is prototype-level

Yahoo adjusted closes are adequate for prototypes but not enough for professional research. The system does not version raw data snapshots, audit corporate action revisions, explicitly handle ETF inception survivorship, or persist downloaded data as part of the run artifact. Missing returns are filled with zero, which can mask data-quality issues. FRED is flagged as not point-in-time safe unless ALFRED vintages are used, but no actual vintage-safe macro pipeline is implemented.

6. Text/macro capabilities are mostly scaffolding

The repository advertises FOMC, Beige Book, SEC EDGAR, and FRED data, and it has useful primitives such as FOMC lexicon scoring and next-day event alignment. But the current strategy uses only price momentum. FOMC ingestion only parses links; Beige Book and SEC modules mostly normalize metadata; FRED only constructs URLs. There is not yet a robust dataset builder, feature store, coverage report, text-model evaluation harness, or integration of these features into candidate strategies.

7. Documentation and tests preserve stale OOS-selection semantics

The conflict between program.md/train.py and README.md/prepare.py::passes_oos_gate/tests/test_oos.py is the most important operational flaw. It creates ambiguity for future autonomous agents and can silently reintroduce test-set leakage.

Recommended upgrades before trusting this for serious quant research

  1. Resolve the objective mismatch. Update README.md, prepare.py::passes_oos_gate, and tests/test_oos.py so validation-CAGR selection is the only keep/discard rule and OOS remains diagnostic.
  2. Add cost sensitivity. Report results at 0, 2, 5, 10, and 25 bps one-way trading cost using actual monthly weight deltas. Include tax/friction notes for retail use.
  3. Add robustness analysis. Run parameter neighborhoods for lookback, top-N, universe subsets, rebalance day, score weighting, risk cap, and cash fallback. Prefer broad robust regions over the top row.
  4. Use walk-forward or combinatorial validation. Add rolling train/validation/OOS folds, or at minimum multiple non-overlapping validation windows with embargoes.
  5. Track more diagnostics without selecting on them. Add max drawdown, annualized volatility, Sharpe, Calmar, worst day/month, CVaR, turnover, number of holdings, and concentration to the ledger.
  6. Version the data. Persist cache manifests with symbol, source URL, download timestamp, row count, first/last date, sha256, and adjusted-close column provenance.
  7. Harden data quality checks. Fail or warn on long missing stretches instead of filling all missing returns with 0.0; handle ETF inception explicitly.
  8. Promote public text datasets from scaffolding to real features. Build actual FOMC/Beige Book/FRED/SEC ingestion artifacts with coverage tests, publication timestamps, lagged alignment, and feature diagnostics.
  9. Archive every formal trial artifact. Store strategy parameters, code hash, data manifest, run log, and result row together so the ledger can be reproduced later.
  10. Add anti-overfit statistical reporting. Include number of trials, family of variants tested, best-vs-median validation performance, and a “selection bias warning” in analysis artifacts.
